Me

Work, etc.

I studied Biomedical Sciences at university. It was interesting and challenging, but the world of academia and research is not for me. I had a great time creating a small Python script to analyse a load of images for my final year project, however!


After university, I joined Brainlabs, a digital advertising company, with the intention of moving into their tech team and learning on the job. Once there, I discovered that, while there were plenty of small scripting tasks to be done, there was a good chance that I wouldn't get much (or any) exposure to design, back-end work or creating a large project in this environment. With this in mind, I started to look for alternatives, and found FAC!


Despite deciding not to move into the tech team at the company, I still managed to challenge myself as an Account Manager; attending several of the tech team's weekly talks, helping colleagues with some JS debugging, and creating a functional mockup landing page for an important client to demonstrate the features and design changes that I had been suggesting to them.


After a little under a year there, I knew that I would eventually leave to pursue web development, and managed to get part-time work in addition to my full-time job to help me save up enough money to leave and pursue this properly.

Why This Course?

I'll keep this brief, as I'm sure it'll come up in the interviews, if I make it!

Values

I love that FAC has clearly managed to foster such a great community, be it in the meetups or the cohorts themselves.


The democratic/meritocratic nature of the application process (and the lack of prohibitive fees) means that anyone lucky enough to get on the course can rest assured that they'll be surrounded by a really talented and diverse group. Who wouldn't be excited about that?!


The emphasis on open source values and "giving back" is also very laudable, and I'd be very excited to be a part of it (see the "opportunities" section).

Opportunities

Spending time mentoring the next cohort, teaching other groups (as I've witnessed at space4, just before the Monday evening meetups) and working on portfolio projects (possibly with non-profits) will be a great way for me to not only give back, but to solidify my own knowledge.


Following on from the above, I'd absolutely love to help mentor in both Nazareth and Gaza after this course.


Finally, dwyl looks like it would be my ideal place to work. I am really keen to get proficient at developing MVPs due to my own longer-term entrepreneurial ambitions. It also looks to be a company that really fosters an environment of learning, e.g. some people at dwyl have pulled together learning resources for Elixir, which is a language I'm planning to pick up after I have the fundamentals and full-stack JS experience.

Syllabus

I'm very keen to learn full-stack JavaScript, and this is one of the few bootcamps that has a focus on this, rather than using Ruby/Rails, which I feel is less "cutting-edge".


More importantly, the peer-led, collaborative, project-focused setup is exactly the sort of environment that I believe I would learn - and help others to learn - well in.

Prerequisites & Project

Prerequisites

Since completing the prerequisites, I've enjoyed helping others get to grips with freeCodeCamp and Codewars at the weekly meetups, and have also created a couple more kata, one of which was quite challenging to actually implement, but helped me to improve my RegEx knowledge and get a glimpse of the value of TDD.


As for fCC, I've completed some of the more advanced scripting challenges, but look forward to completing them all over the coming weeks. I also had a great time making my podcast project, which was inspired by a couple of the ones on the fCC curriculum.


Aside from fCC, Codewars and reading around in general, I've been dipping my toes in C and CompSci fundamentals via EdX's CS50, and keeping my JS sharp thanks to tutorials from Wes Bos and "The Net Ninja". I also recently started to use Scrimba, created by FAC's own Per Borgen!

Podcast Project

I absolutely love podcasts. It's great to feel like I'm learning during "dead time" like commuting and cleaning, and to give my eyes a rest. As you'll see in the readme for the project repo, I've listened to a lot of them, and am subscribed to a healthy 140!


I saw the fCC "Random Quote Machine" and "Weather API" challenges, and thought I would merge them with my love of podcasts to make my Random Podcast Machine.


It's not the prettiest, but it's responsive and written without any libraries or frameworks. It also has a tweet functionality, history displayed at the bottom, and some great podcast recommendations covering topics like coding, news, politics and technology. This is just a small sample of my podcast subscriptions, so feel free to ask for more recommendations :).
